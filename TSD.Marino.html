Who doesn't love a good History channel documentary?¶

When I was a kid, I watched documentaries all the time. One of the most common topics on the History channel was the possibility of a catastrophic disaster and what the consequences would look like in today's world. The data collected in this set reminds me of these documentaries, but brings in a more realistic take on how the population feels about the idea of a minor or massive earthquake taking place. This data set takes a look at the population of the US and asks questions pertaining to the threat of the San Andreas fault annd earthquakes in general in everyday life. There are a total of 1,014 responses.

1. How does familiarity with the San Andreas fault line affect nervousness about the possibility of a big, catastrophic earthquake?¶

My first question focues on familiarity and nervousness of the general population in terms of the possibility of a large earthquake or natural disaster. The results are shown below.

In [5]:
#Familiarity and Nervousness
question1 = pd.crosstab(earthquakes['How familiar are you with the San Andreas Fault line?'], earthquakes['How worried are you about the Big One, a massive, catastrophic earthquake?'])

#Visualization
question1 = question1.cumsum()
plt.clf()
question1.plot(kind = 'barh', figsize = (10,10), colormap = 'autumn')
plt.title('Familiarity and Nervousness')
plt.xlabel('Frequency of Answer')

As seen above, the more familiar one is with the fault line and its capabilities, the more nervous they are about the possibility of it actually occuring, along with the effects that would come with it. Intriguingly, most people (no matter how familiar with the fault line) are categorized in "Not at all worried" or "Not so worried" about a large event occuring in the near future. Few people are "Extremely familiar" with the San Andreas fault, but those are resignate with the "Extremely worried" category in a higher percentage than those who are any of the other familiarities.

Problems with Data¶

Although the data asked good, detailed questions of the participants, the survey only had 1,014 answers that were spread out across the country. This is not a good, varied number for amount of people who are affected by the idea of earthquakes. Therefore, the values given may not be reflective of the entire population.

Summary¶

The data shows that people are greatly affected by the San Andreas fault and the earthquakes that come with it. Whether it's the threat of a massive earthquake in one's lifetime or the probibility of experiencing even a minor earthquake, people are interested and intimidated by the power of these natural occurances. The subject of a catastrophic event is popular in the US culture, as it seems like an impossibility. That's what makes it interesting.
